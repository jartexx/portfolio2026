Approach

How I Design

Design Thinking was my gospel. Empathise, define, ideate, prototype, test. A structured path I trusted completely. Then AI changed the pace, and the framework stopped being the point. Discovery happens in production now. Vision travels through working prototypes, not decks. The loop between design and engineering collapsed. My base didn't change. How tightly I hold it did.

◇ 01

Discover

Talk to stakeholders. Dig into the data. But don't wait for it to be perfect. Discovery happens in production too. Real users interacting with real systems reveal what no research brief ever could.

◇ 02

Define

Map the flows. Clarify direction. But static Figma screens stopped being the primary communication tool. A working prototype says more than any deck, faster.

◇ 03

Specify

Write the PRD. Every requirement, every edge case, every state. The spec still matters, but it follows the prototype now, not the other way around. What you can articulate is what gets built well.

◇ 04

Generate

Drop the spec into Claude Code or Figma Make. Weeks collapse into hours. AI is getting better at taste, but what actually matters is still my call. That judgment stays mine.

◇ 05

Ship & Iterate

Ship. Watch what happens. AI systems don't behave the same way twice. You can't mock every state in advance. Real use cases only emerge from real data. The loop is the validation.

CASE STUDY 01

E-Commerce Platform Migration

Re-platforming a 13-store Latino retail operation from Magento to Shopify Plus, and converting a post-migration traffic drop into a profitability breakthrough.

RoleLead UX Strategist & Architect
Timeline8 months
Year2024–2025
TeamCross-functional, 12 people

The Challenge

Curacao isn't your average retailer. 13 stores across California, Nevada, and Arizona, serving Latino families who often can't get a credit card anywhere else. Their in-house financing program, CPay, is how customers buy a TV, build credit, and come back. That trust is the actual product.

The problem: their Magento site hadn't kept up. Every update needed a dev ticket. In a business that refreshes campaigns every two weeks, that's not slow. That's broken.

But the bigger issue wasn't the platform. It was the architecture underneath. The site was organized like the company's internal departments, not how a customer thinks when they go shopping online. I benchmarked Walmart, Best Buy, and Amazon to prove it.

Before/after site map comparison

What I Found

"Beauty & Men's Grooming" was buried inside Fashion. "Kids & Babies" mixed bassinets with video gaming consoles. Nobody else organized it that way, because customers don't shop that way.

Every change I made was backed by what the benchmark showed. Toys & Gaming became its own department. Baby became its own. Health & Beauty moved out of Fashion and became a top-level destination. Simple moves, real difference in how people find things.

How I Approached It

  1. Competitive Benchmark. Mapped how Walmart, Best Buy, and Amazon structured their categories. Used that as the baseline, not Curacao's org chart.
  2. IA Restructuring. Rebuilt the navigation around how customers shop, not how the company is organized. Documented every decision so the external dev agency could actually build it.
  3. Component-Based Design System. Built a modular UI library so the marketing team could run campaigns, update catalogs, and manage promotions on their own. No more dev tickets for a banner change.
Component library & design system overview

The Hard Parts

Agency coordination: We didn't have in-house dev capacity for this, so a third-party agency built it. The problem: everything Curacao knew about their own system lived in people's heads. I had to design the system and document it at the same time, writing requirements that had never been written before, for a team that had never worked with Curacao.

Shopify vs. Curacao's custom logic: Shopify doesn't do custom checkouts. CPay, Curacao's in-house financing, had to live in checkout. The fix was building a dedicated CPay Shopify app that plugged into native checkout without replacing it. Then there was the Club Membership program. Its discount logic was complex enough that it pushed against Shopify's theme limits too. Every constraint needed a solution that didn't break the platform.

Scope: Ten years of Magento custom code. Every feature had to be evaluated: rebuild it, replace it, or cut it. The answer was different every time.

What Changed

Before: every campaign update needed a dev ticket. After: the e-commerce team handled it themselves. Bi-weekly campaigns, catalog updates, promotions, all without touching dev. Developer involvement in routine work dropped by about 50%. For a business that moves at retail speed, that's everything.

Post-Launch: The Login Problem

We launched without SSO. It came months later. That meant thousands of existing customers hit a new platform with no account on it. I mapped what that actually looked like: four distinct states, each with a different resolution.

  1. My Account holders (customers with active financing). Had a Magento account with an active CPay credit line, but no Shopify account. The most sensitive case: these customers had a financial relationship with Curacao, and losing their login meant losing access to payment history and credit tools.
  2. Non-My Account holders. Had a legacy Magento account (purchase history, no credit) but no Shopify account. Key risk: these users might attempt a password reset on the My Account credit portal instead of the Shopify storefront, triggering the wrong system entirely. Account state detected: disabled / invited / declined.
  3. Customers with an existing Shopify account. The simplest case. Account state: enabled. Standard login flow, no conflicts.
  4. New customers creating an account. Where the system could detect a pre-existing Shopify record from a prior transaction, surfacing a confusing "email already in use" state with no explanation.

Every state needed its own flow: what the user sees, what the system knows, what happens next. Getting it wrong meant customers couldn't access their financing history. Getting it right meant the transition was invisible.

The Results

MetricBeforeAfterChange
Bounce Rate42%32.67%-22%
ROAS (Q4)$21$29.18+39% YoY
Revenue (Oct)Baseline+6.6% YoY↑ Despite lower traffic
Dev dependencyHighNear-zeroTeam autonomy

Traffic dropped 38% after launch. We knew it would. Re-platforming always does that, and the paid budget got cut hard that year too. But revenue still grew and ROAS hit all-time highs. Less traffic, more money. We stopped buying people who weren't going to buy.

CASE STUDY 02

Miden Crypto Wallet

A design pitch for Fat Solutions. Making Zero-Knowledge privacy feel like sending a text message, not running a cryptographic protocol.

RoleLead Product Designer
TypeAgency Pitch
AgencyFat Solutions
ClientMiden (Polygon)
Timeline2 months
TeamCore team of 5

The Context

Fat Solutions brought me in on a pitch. The client: Miden, a privacy-first wallet running on the Polygon blockchain. The ask: make Zero-Knowledge privacy accessible to people who've never heard the words "Zero-Knowledge." Two months. Let's go.

The Challenge

ZK proofs are remarkable. Transactions verified without exposing any underlying data. Privacy at the protocol level, built in, not bolted on. The problem: every wallet in the space designs like the user has a computer science degree. The complexity surfaces everywhere: in the terminology, the flows, the error states. It shuts people out before they ever get started.

The Competitive Landscape

The benchmark covered App Store and Google Play data across four wallets (Feb 2024–Feb 2025). The pattern: the market leader carries significant UX debt, and the alternatives either fail on mobile or lack the adoption to validate their approach at scale.

WalletScore (Android / iOS)Avg StarsReviewsKey Signal
MetaMask72 / 653.6 / 3.321,640+~5,500 one-star reviews (~26%), dominant but deeply frustrating
Phantom81 / 534.0 / 2.710,805+Strong Android; iOS feels like a different, worse product
Sentz73 / 533.7 / 2.7~604Same iOS gap as Phantom, mobile is the industry's blind spot

MetaMask owns the market. It also has 26% one-star reviews. Phantom is the best experience on Android and falls apart on iOS. The smaller players haven't proven anything at scale yet. The gap was obvious: nobody had built a privacy wallet that actually respected the user's time.

The Insight

The breakthrough was reframing what a ZK note actually is. It's not a cryptographic object. It's a message. A private one. Public: share your address. Private: generate a one-time ZK note, send it through WhatsApp, iMessage, Telegram, whatever the recipient already uses. Privacy becomes how you send, not a setting you configure first.

Worked this out with Fat Solutions' engineering team. Once we had the mental model right, the rest of the design snapped into place.

↑ Competitive analysis — Phantom · Payy · Sentz · MetaMask

What I Did

  1. Competitive Benchmark. Pulled App Store and Google Play data across four wallets over 12 months. Mapped sentiment, scores, review volume. The pattern kept repeating: technical complexity surfaces exactly where trust should be.
  2. Mental Model Work. Miden calls transaction objects "Notes." That word already does the work. A Note is something you send privately to someone. Built the whole UX around that metaphor and didn't look back.
  3. End-to-End Flow Design. Three flows, two modes each. Onboarding, Send, Receive. Public and private variants for all of them. Every scenario in the system, designed and documented.

The Three Flows

Onboarding. Two paths: new wallet, or import an existing one. Both involve the State File, the cryptographic record of your wallet state. New user: set password → wallet created → download your State File. Required, not optional. The challenge: making "download this cryptographic backup file" feel like a normal step, not a warning sign you'd skip on instinct.

Send. The decision happens first. Private or public? Private: pick a token, enter an amount, a Note is generated, then you choose how to deliver it. Pick your messaging app. Choose your contact. Done. Public: token, amount, address. Standard crypto, untouched. Privacy isn't a feature you find later. It's the first question.

Receive. Mirror of Send. Public: QR code, copy address, share. Private: enter the Note manually or upload the file. Note imported, transaction complete. The mental model is the same on both ends. That was deliberate. Consistency is how trust gets built.

↑ MVP user flows — Onboarding · Send · Receive

The Core Technical Challenge

When you send a private Note, you're generating a cryptographic object, submitting it to the blockchain, and waiting for chain sync. None of that should be visible to the user. The whole job was progressive disclosure. Surface the intent, hide the protocol. Make the complexity load-bearing but invisible. That's the entire game with ZK UX.

The Outcome

The pitch landed. Specific call-out: flow clarity given the technical constraints. A ZK wallet has more edge cases per screen than almost anything else I've worked on: public/private states, chain sync states, file handling. We designed all of them. The feedback: clean, navigable, and appropriate for what's running underneath. That was the whole goal.

CASE STUDY 03

Tires & Rims Ecosystem

Designing a five-party logistics chain into a seamless purchase-to-installation experience, from Year/Make/Model search to signed delivery proof.

RoleUX Lead & Service Designer
Timeline5 months
TeamCross-functional, 6 people
StatusSoft Launch Q2 2026

The Challenge

Curacao wanted their tire business online. Sounds like an e-commerce project. It wasn't. Tires don't ship to your door. They end up on a car lift, after passing through five parties, two systems, and a documentation chain that has to be airtight before anyone gets paid.

The full chain: customer selects tires → Curacao orders from ATD (American Tire Distributors) → ATD ships to installer → installer is notified with arrival details → appointment scheduled → tires installed → installer submits signed documentation and photos → Curacao processes the invoice. Every handoff automatic. Every party with their own view. This wasn't a storefront problem. It was a full operational system.

Service blueprint, full logistics chain

The Four Users

Four distinct users. All connected. The end customer wants to find tires for their car and book an appointment. That's their whole world. ATD needs clean order data routed to the right installer location, no surprises. The local installer needs to know what's arriving, when, who the customer is, and exactly what to do with it. Curacao's operations team needs full chain visibility and a way to process invoices the moment the installer submits proof of work. Design for all four simultaneously, or the system doesn't hold.

What I Did

  1. Benchmark the Search Model. Costco Tires was the reference. They'd already solved this: customers arrive knowing different things. Some have a license plate. Some know year and make. Some have a tire size on a napkin. The design supports four entry points: License Plate + State, Vehicle (Year/Make/Model/Trim), Tire Size, and VIN. Meet people where they are.
  2. Logic-First Interaction Design. The Year → Make → Model → Trim funnel is sequentially gated by API constraints. Each field only unlocks once the previous is confirmed. That's a real system requirement, not a design choice. The challenge: make that feel like guidance, not a form that locks you out. Every constraint is real. None of it should feel like friction.
  3. Design the Ecosystem, Not Just the Storefront. The B2B installer portal is the other half of this product. It's where tires show up, appointments get scheduled, and proof of installation gets submitted. That submission triggers invoicing. The portal and storefront were designed as one connected system, because they are one.
Filter logic flows & B2B dashboard

The Result

A customer buys tires. That's all they do. Behind that: Curacao sends an order to ATD, ATD routes it to the right installer, the installer gets notified automatically, schedules the appointment, installs the tires, submits signed documentation and photos. Curacao processes the invoice. No one had to manually coordinate any of it.

Soft launch is Q2 2026. First time Curacao's tire business runs as a fully automated, connected ecosystem. We designed the whole chain, then made it invisible to the people using it.
